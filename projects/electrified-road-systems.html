Sustainable Vehicle Design

Electrified Road Systems

Sustainability and implementation study of dynamic road charging for heavy-duty transport.

Back to Projects

Overview

This project evaluated electrified road systems as a way to reduce range anxiety, charging downtime, and battery size in long-haul electric transport. The study compared overhead catenary, conductive ground rail, and inductive road-charging concepts, then connected the technical assessment to environmental, economic, and social sustainability.

The public summary focuses on technology selection, life-cycle impact, and deployment trade-offs. Detailed assumptions, calculations, and citations remain in the full report.

Work Performed

  • Compared catenary, conductive rail, and inductive ERS architectures for vehicle compatibility and infrastructure complexity.
  • Reviewed pilot implementations including Swedish ERS roads, German eHighway sections, and wireless road-charging demonstrations.
  • Assessed sustainability across social acceptance, environmental impact, and long-term economic feasibility.
  • Built a simplified life-cycle assessment for a catenary ERS corridor using battery, grid, infrastructure, operation, maintenance, and recycling assumptions.
  • Evaluated implementation trade-offs such as installation cost, vehicle hardware changes, scalability, visual impact, and grid integration.

Key Insights

  • ERS is most promising as a range extender on high-traffic freight corridors rather than as a blanket solution for every road.
  • Battery downsizing from a long-range pack to an ERS-assisted pack can reduce vehicle mass, raw-material demand, and battery manufacturing emissions.
  • The simplified catenary case showed low vehicle operation emissions under Sweden's low-carbon grid, while infrastructure operation and maintenance dominated corridor-level CO2 impact.
  • Catenary systems are mature for heavy vehicles, but carry visual and installation trade-offs; conductive and inductive systems are less visually intrusive but introduce their own alignment, cost, and efficiency constraints.
  • Large-scale deployment depends on public acceptance, transparent cost sharing, traffic density, and interoperability between road authorities and vehicle systems.

Summary

The project positions ERS as a complementary technology for decarbonising heavy-duty transport. It can support smaller batteries and lower operational emissions when paired with clean electricity, but its strongest case depends on corridor utilisation, infrastructure planning, and the chosen charging architecture. The study also highlighted the need for better empirical data from active pilot roads before drawing firm conclusions across all ERS types.

Tools Life-cycle assessment, literature review, sustainability analysis
Technologies Catenary ERS, conductive rail, inductive charging
Focus Heavy-duty transport, battery downsizing, infrastructure trade-offs
