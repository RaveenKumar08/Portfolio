Vehicle Acoustics and Vibrations

Vehicle Acoustics

Fluid-structure interaction study of cabin acoustic modes, firewall dynamics, and forced response.

Back to Projects

Overview

This project studied a simplified body-in-white acoustic cavity using MATLAB-based modal analysis. The work examined uncoupled and coupled eigenfrequencies, then moved into forced response studies to understand how firewall and rear-door dynamics influence sound pressure level at the driver's ear.

The page summarises the engineering conclusions from the assignment while keeping the full equation derivations and detailed MATLAB output inside the linked reports.

Work Performed

  • Analysed uncoupled acoustic and structural eigenmodes for the passenger compartment, firewall, and rear door.
  • Studied coupled fluid-structure modes for the firewall-only case and for the firewall plus rear-door case.
  • Identified zero-pressure locations and dominant acoustic and structural modal participation factors.
  • Evaluated forced response using SPL at the driver's ear and displacement response of the firewall.
  • Varied firewall thickness to study the interaction between mass, bending stiffness, frequency shift, and SPL response.
  • Compared firewall-only response with firewall and rear-door coupled response to understand persistent structural mode contributions.

Key Results

  • The first non-zero uncoupled acoustic eigenfrequency was 87.3508 Hz, with a zero-pressure location at 0.9831 m from the firewall.
  • Coupling the firewall shifted the first modes to 78.8352 Hz, 96.2712 Hz, and 132.0254 Hz because the fluid adds effective inertia to the structure.
  • Adding the rear door produced coupled modes at 75.36 Hz, 87.328 Hz, and 100.2031 Hz, with firewall mode 67 and rear-door mode 72 appearing persistently.
  • The firewall forced-response case had first acoustic peaks near 78.85 Hz and 96.3 Hz, with a highest SPL case around 135 dB at 349.875 Hz.
  • Reducing firewall thickness lowered BIW mass to 86.8664 kg and raised the peak SPL, while increasing thickness raised BIW mass to 99.1124 kg and shifted response peaks upward.
  • The rear-door study showed that structural modes 67 and 72 remained important across both low-frequency acoustic peaks and the maximum SPL case.

Summary

The study showed how strongly cabin acoustics depend on structural coupling. The uncoupled acoustic modes provide a useful baseline, but adding firewall and rear-door flexibility shifts the response, changes zero-pressure locations, and introduces persistent structural modes. Firewall thickness also changes the balance between mass and stiffness, so NVH design needs both modal participation and SPL response rather than frequency targets alone.
