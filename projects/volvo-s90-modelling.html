Vehicle Modelling and Validation

Volvo S90 Vehicle Modelling

Multi-body vehicle model development and validation using ADAMS Car and MATLAB.

Back to Projects Contact

Overview

This project developed and validated a Volvo S90 vehicle dynamics model using ADAMS Car for multi-body simulation and MATLAB for analytical bicycle-model comparison. The work covered suspension, steering, tyre, brake, powertrain, and full-vehicle assembly setup, followed by validation against VBOX track-test data from manoeuvres such as step steer, ramp steer, slalom, and double lane change.

The model was used to study roll, yaw, and lateral acceleration behaviour, tune tyre parameters, and evaluate how mass and centre-of-gravity changes influence vehicle handling response.

Work Performed

  • Created ADAMS Car templates, subsystems, and assemblies for the Volvo S90 vehicle model.
  • Modelled front double wishbone and rear multi-link suspension assemblies with anti-roll bars.
  • Ran parallel and opposite wheel-travel tests and compared wheel-force results with analytical calculations.
  • Built a MATLAB bicycle model with linear and non-linear tyre representations for reference validation.
  • Preprocessed VBOX measurement data and used it as driver input for ADAMS and MATLAB simulations.
  • Validated roll rate, roll angle, lateral acceleration, and yaw rate across steering manoeuvres.
  • Tuned MATLAB tyre stiffness values and ADAMS tyre scaling factors to improve model correlation.
  • Performed a parameter study on vehicle mass and centre-of-gravity position using the validated model.

Summary

The Volvo S90 model was developed through a modular ADAMS Car workflow. Front and rear suspension templates were first converted into working subsystems, combined with anti-roll bars, and checked through wheel-travel simulations. The front double wishbone suspension showed close agreement with analytical force estimates, while the rear multi-link assembly showed larger differences because the analytical comparison used a simplified double wishbone approximation.

A complete vehicle assembly was then evaluated alongside a MATLAB bicycle model. VBOX test data from Lunda was converted and preprocessed before being used for validation, including steering offset correction and compatible input preparation for simulation. Step steer and ramp steer tests confirmed that the ADAMS model captured the main roll-rate and roll-angle trends, while slalom and double lane change runs were used to compare lateral acceleration and yaw response.

Tyre tuning was an important part of the validation process. MATLAB cornering stiffness values were adjusted to improve agreement with measured data, and ADAMS tyre scaling factors were studied to refine yaw and lateral acceleration response. The validated model was finally used for a parameter study, showing that reduced mass increased agility but also oscillation, higher mass produced smoother but slower response, higher CoG increased roll angle, and rearward CoG increased yaw response with a stability trade-off.
